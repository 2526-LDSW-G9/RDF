In the Netherlands, nature is not just a backdrop to urban development — it is a legal constraint on it. Under the Wet natuurbescherming (Nature Protection Act), the presence of protected species in or around a building site can halt a project entirely, or require costly mitigation before a permit is granted. Two groups of species are especially consequential in Twente: owls (Strigiformes) and bats, in particular the Gewone dwergvleermuis (Pipistrellus pipistrellus).

This data story queries a linked biodiversity dataset of occurrence records from Twente, federating with Wikidata to resolve taxonomic lineage. Each query is live — results are fetched directly from the triple store and plotted on the map below.

"A bat roosting in a cavity wall is not an inconvenience — it is a legally protected resident."

Owl Sightings in Twente — Strigiformes

Strigiformes — the order of owls — are strictly protected under both Dutch law and the European Habitats Directive. Species such as the Kerkuil (barn owl) and Ransuil (long-eared owl) frequently roost in agricultural buildings, church towers, and woodland edges. A confirmed nesting site within a development zone can trigger a mandatory ecological survey and, in many cases, a multi-year delay.

The query below retrieves all owl occurrence records from the dataset, cross-referencing Wikidata to resolve Dutch, English, Chinese, and Kannada common names. Results are deduplicated on vernacular name — use the table to browse species, and the map to explore the spatial distribution of sightings.

Wikidata · Q25222

Strigiformes — the owl order. The query traverses wdt:P171* (parent taxon, transitive) to capture all species within this clade present in the dataset.

Query 1 · Owl Occurrences — Strigiformes (Q25222) wlw/sparql
· · ·

The results show the distinct owl species recorded in the area. Because this query uses SELECT DISTINCT on vernacular names rather than individual occurrences, it gives a clean species inventory rather than a point cloud — useful for a first assessment of which taxa are present.

Dwergvleermuis Sightings — The Bat That Blocks Planning

The Gewone dwergvleermuis (Pipistrellus pipistrellus) is the most common bat in the Netherlands, with an estimated population of 300,000 to 600,000 individuals. Despite its abundance, it is fully protected under the Wet natuurbescherming — and its preference for roosting in cavity walls, roof spaces, and building joints makes it a routine obstacle in urban development and renovation projects throughout Twente.

Any construction or demolition work that could disturb a roost requires a species survey and, if bats are present, an exemption (ontheffing) from the provincial authority. This process typically takes several months and may require compensatory measures such as installing bat boxes or redesigning the building envelope.

The query below retrieves all Chiroptera (bat) occurrences from the dataset — specifically those under Wikidata entity Q176901 — with their georeferenced observation points. Switch to the 🗺 Map tab to see where in Twente bat activity has been recorded.

Wikidata · Q176901

Chiroptera — the bat order. All species in this clade recorded as dwc:Occurrence in the dataset are included, joined on dwc:scientificName via the Wikidata federation.

Query 2 · Bat Occurrences with Geometry — Chiroptera (Q176901) wlw/sparql
· · ·

Each point on the map represents a georeferenced bat observation. Clusters of sightings near built-up areas are particularly significant from a planning perspective — these are precisely the locations where roost surveys would be required before any demolition or renovation permit could be issued.

The GeoJSON geometry for each occurrence is stored via wlw:hasObservationArea/geo:hasGeometry/geo:asWKT and rendered here using Leaflet with an OpenStreetMap base layer.

Spatial Precision — Grid Cells & Blurring Levels

Not all occurrence records are published at full spatial resolution. Sensitive species — particularly those vulnerable to disturbance at nesting or roosting sites — are deliberately blurred to a coarser grid cell before public release. This practice is standard in Dutch biodiversity data management and is governed by the data provider's blurring policy.

The query below joins each observation to its grid cell (wlw:intersectsGridCell) and its blurring level (wlw:hasBlurringLevel), giving a direct view of which species groups have their precise locations withheld and at what coarseness.

wlw:BlurringLevel

Blurring levels are encoded as named individuals in the local ontology. The STRAFTER function strips the namespace prefix so the table displays the short label directly (e.g. Blurred1km, Blurred5km).

Query 3 · Observations — Grid Cell & Blurring Level wlw/sparql
· · ·

Comparing the blurring column across species groups reveals which taxa are considered most sensitive. Records with a coarser grid cell and a higher blurring level are precisely those that would require direct fieldwork to verify location for a planning assessment.

Survey Methods — Observation Protocols

Occurrence records are collected through a variety of standardised survey protocols — ranging from passive acoustic bat detectors and nest-box inspections to transect walks and citizen-science submissions. The protocol used affects both the detectability of a species and the legal weight of the observation in a permit application.

This query links each observation to its wlw:usesProtocol relation and surfaces the human-readable wlw:protocolName. Browsing the results by species group makes it immediately clear which taxa are monitored through structured surveys versus opportunistic records.

wlw:ObservationProtocol

Protocols are modelled as first-class entities in the ontology, allowing multiple observations to share a single protocol instance and enabling protocol-level aggregations without string matching.

Query 4 · Observations — Survey Protocol wlw/sparql
· · ·

Records gathered through standardised, repeatable protocols carry more evidential weight in an ecological assessment than one-off opportunistic sightings. Where a protocol name is absent, the observation was either submitted without protocol metadata or predates the ontology's protocol vocabulary.

5

Temporal Coverage — Observation Periods

Many ecological surveys are conducted across a defined period rather than on a single date — a bat detector might run for an entire night, or a grid-cell survey might span a full breeding season. The dataset models this through a dedicated observation period entity with explicit start and end timestamps.

The query below retrieves the wlw:periodStart and wlw:periodEnd for each observation, alongside its species group. This allows a temporal overview: which groups are surveyed seasonally, and whether recent years show increasing or decreasing monitoring effort.

wlw:ObservationPeriod

Period start and end are stored as typed xsd:date literals, making them directly sortable and filterable in SPARQL without string parsing.

Query 5 · Observations — Period Start & End wlw/sparql
· · ·

Long survey windows covering spring and summer correspond to the breeding season assessments required under the Wet natuurbescherming. Short overnight periods are characteristic of acoustic bat surveys. The spread of dates in the results gives a direct picture of the dataset's temporal completeness.

6

Combined View — Policy, Spatial & Protocol

A planning assessment does not consider any one dimension in isolation: the legal protection status of a species, the spatial precision of its recorded locations, and the survey method used to detect it all feed into the same decision. This query brings those three dimensions together in a single result set.

Each row shows one observation with its species group, policy status (resolved from the ontology's named individuals via COALESCE), grid cell, blurring level, and protocol — giving the most complete per-observation picture available in the dataset.

wlw:PolicyStatus

Policy status is attached at the species level, not the observation level, so a single lookup on wlw:hasPolicyStatus propagates to all occurrences of that taxon. The COALESCE expression prefers a human-readable label but falls back to the local name of the IRI.

Query 6 · Combined — Policy · Grid · Blurring · Protocol wlw/sparql
· · ·

Filtering this table on a high-protection policy status and a coarse blurring level immediately surfaces the observations where a developer faces the greatest regulatory exposure: legally sensitive species whose precise locations are withheld from the public record.

7

Administrative Geography — Grid Cells & Regions

Dutch nature-protection assessments are often scoped by administrative region — municipality, province, or a designated Natura 2000 area boundary. The dataset links each observation area to both a standardised grid cell and an administrative region, enabling cross-referencing between the national monitoring grid and the jurisdictional boundaries that govern permitting decisions.

This query retrieves the grid cell identifier and the region name for each observation, giving a direct join table between the two spatial reference systems used in the dataset. It is particularly useful for aggregating species counts per municipality or identifying which grid cells fall within a contested planning area.

wlw:ObservationArea

Observation areas are modelled as spatial objects that can simultaneously intersectsGridCell and intersectsRegion, reflecting the real-world situation where a single survey plot straddles multiple administrative boundaries.

Query 7 · Observations — Grid Cell & Administrative Region wlw/sparql
· · ·

Where both a grid cell and a region name are present, the observation can be placed precisely within the administrative geography used for permit review. Records with a region but no grid cell — or vice versa — indicate gaps in the spatial annotation that may need to be resolved before the data can support a formal planning assessment.
