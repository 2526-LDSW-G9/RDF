In the Netherlands, nature is not just a backdrop to urban development — it is a legal constraint on it. Under the Wet natuurbescherming (Nature Protection Act), the presence of protected species in or around a building site can halt a project entirely, or require costly mitigation before a permit is granted. Two groups of species are especially consequential in Twente: owls (Strigiformes) and bats, in particular the Gewone dwergvleermuis (Pipistrellus pipistrellus).

This data story queries a linked biodiversity dataset of occurrence records from Twente. The four queries below progress in complexity: from a simple local query, to a richer multi-join on local data, then to federation with Wikidata for taxonomic lineage, and finally a triple federation that also reaches the Kadaster Knowledge Graph (KKG) to resolve a municipal boundary by name. Each query is live — results are fetched directly from the triple store.

"A bat roosting in a cavity wall is not an inconvenience — it is a legally protected resident."

Survey Methods — Observation Protocols

Occurrence records are collected through a variety of standardised survey protocols — ranging from passive acoustic bat detectors and nest-box inspections to transect walks and citizen-science submissions. The protocol used affects both the detectability of a species and the legal weight of the observation in a permit application.

This query links each observation to its wlw:usesProtocol relation and surfaces the human-readable wlw:protocolName. It touches only the local triple store — no federation — making it the most direct query in this story. Browsing the results by species group makes it immediately clear which taxa are monitored through structured surveys versus opportunistic records.

wlw:ObservationProtocol

Protocols are modelled as first-class entities in the ontology, allowing multiple observations to share a single protocol instance and enabling protocol-level aggregations without string matching.

Query 1 · Observations — Survey Protocol wlw/sparql · local
· · ·

Records gathered through standardised, repeatable protocols carry more evidential weight in an ecological assessment than one-off opportunistic sightings. Where a protocol name is absent, the observation was either submitted without protocol metadata or predates the ontology's protocol vocabulary.

Combined View — Policy, Spatial & Protocol

A planning assessment does not consider any one dimension in isolation: the legal protection status of a species, the spatial precision of its recorded locations, and the survey method used to detect it all feed into the same decision. This query brings those three dimensions together in a single result set — still entirely within the local triple store, but joining across four optional paths simultaneously.

Each row shows one observation with its species group, policy status (resolved from the ontology's named individuals via COALESCE), grid cell, blurring level, and protocol — giving the most complete per-observation picture available in the dataset.

wlw:PolicyStatus

Policy status is attached at the species level, not the observation level, so a single lookup on wlw:hasPolicyStatus propagates to all occurrences of that taxon. The COALESCE expression prefers a human-readable label but falls back to the local name of the IRI.

Filtering this table on a high-protection policy status and a coarse blurring level immediately surfaces the observations where a developer faces the greatest regulatory exposure: legally sensitive species whose precise locations are withheld from the public record.

Dwergvleermuis Sightings — The Bat That Blocks Planning

The Gewone dwergvleermuis (Pipistrellus pipistrellus) is the most common bat in the Netherlands, with an estimated population of 300,000 to 600,000 individuals. Despite its abundance, it is fully protected under the Wet natuurbescherming — and its preference for roosting in cavity walls, roof spaces, and building joints makes it a routine obstacle in urban development and renovation projects throughout Twente.

Any construction or demolition work that could disturb a roost requires a species survey and, if bats are present, an exemption (ontheffing) from the provincial authority. This process typically takes several months and may require compensatory measures such as installing bat boxes or redesigning the building envelope.

This query introduces Wikidata federation: the SERVICE clause resolves all species within Chiroptera (Q176901) via the transitive parent-taxon property, then joins back to the local store on scientific name to retrieve georeferenced observations. Switch to the Map tab to see where in Twente bat activity has been recorded.

Wikidata · Q176901

Chiroptera — the bat order. All species in this clade recorded as dwc:Occurrence in the dataset are included, joined on dwc:scientificName via the Wikidata federation.

Query 3 · Bat Occurrences with Geometry — Chiroptera (Q176901) wlw/sparql · federation: Wikidata
· · ·

Each point on the map represents a georeferenced bat observation. Clusters of sightings near built-up areas are particularly significant from a planning perspective — these are precisely the locations where roost surveys would be required before any demolition or renovation permit could be issued.

The GeoJSON geometry for each occurrence is stored via wlw:hasObservationArea/geo:hasGeometry/geo:asWKT and rendered here using Leaflet with an OpenStreetMap base layer.

Owl Counts in Tubbergen — Strigiformes via KKG & Wikidata

The most demanding query in this story federates across three endpoints simultaneously: the local triple store, Wikidata, and the Kadaster Knowledge Graph (KKG) — the authoritative Dutch government linked-data service for administrative and topographic reference data.

The query asks: for each owl species, how many occurrences fall within the municipality of Tubbergen? To answer this, it must (1) retrieve Tubbergen's exact boundary polygon from KKG using the municipality name, (2) resolve all Strigiformes species from Wikidata using the transitive parent-taxon relation, and (3) filter local occurrence geometries with geof:sfWithin against the KKG boundary — grouping by English common name and ordering by count.

This is exactly the kind of query a permit assessor needs: not just "are owls present in Twente" but "how many owl sightings are recorded inside this specific municipality's boundary?"

KKG · Tubbergen / Wikidata · Q25222

KKG provides the imxgeo:Gemeentegebied geometry for Tubbergen. Wikidata provides the owl clade (Strigiformes, Q25222) via wdt:P171*. The geof:sfWithin function then clips local occurrences to that boundary.

Query 4 · Owl Counts in Tubbergen — Strigiformes (Q25222) wlw/sparql · federation: Wikidata + KKG
· · ·

The result ranks owl species by observation count within Tubbergen's administrative boundary. A high count for a particular species in a municipality with active development pressure is a direct flag for the permit review process — it signals that a full species impact assessment is likely required before any construction can proceed.
